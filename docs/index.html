Real series don't fit one box

A series can be stationary, trending, cointegrated, nonlinear — even chaotic. The classical workflow makes you decide which before you can model: test for unit roots, choose how many times to difference, pick a lag order, choose a VAR variant or a VECM and its cointegration rank. A maze of decisions, each a chance to get it wrong.

Symbolic Pure-Time Least Squares (SPTLS) removes the maze. It is ordinary least squares — moved from lagged values to the kinematic jet.

From lagged values to the jet

Instead of regressing on past values, SPTLS regresses on the instantaneous state of the trajectory — level, velocity and acceleration:

q(t) = ( z, Δz, Δ²z )

One closed-form least-squares solve on q(t) keeps everything OLS gives you — speed, a closed form, fully readable coefficients — while the jet lets that same solve describe motion a lagged-value model never can. Add the quadratic terms of the jet and it captures nonlinear vector fields exactly.

Recover the Lorenz attractor — live

The butterfly above is the Lorenz system: deterministic but chaotic, and quadratic. We fit its full 3-D state one step ahead, in your browser, two ways — a linear VAR, and SPTLS on the quadratic jet — then let each fitted model run free from one starting point. Only the model that captured the quadratic field regenerates the attractor.

SPTLS — quadratic jet

recovers the field → regenerates both wings

VAR — linear

can't represent xy, xz → collapses to a point

One-step fit is good for both (Lorenz is locally smooth). The difference shows under free iteration: SPTLS recovered the exact quadratic vector field, so its free-run lands back on the attractor; the linear model cannot, and decays to a fixed point.

Why it works: it's OLS, extended

Because SPTLS is least squares on the jet, it inherits OLS's virtues and extends their reach to the whole zoo of trajectories.
