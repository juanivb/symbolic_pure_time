One idea: model the motion, not the past values

Classical time-series models regress on past values. Symbolic Pure-Time Least Squares (SPTLS) regresses on the instantaneous state of the trajectory — its level, velocity and acceleration, the kinematic jet:

q(t) = ( z, Δz, Δ²z )

Why this domain — a thread from Hamilton and Clifford

Two states of a moving system are compared not by subtracting numbers but by the geometric relation that carries one into the other — a rotation-and-stretch. That relation is exactly what quaternions were built to express: Hamilton's i, j, k are the three planes in which a state can turn. The kinematic jet plays the same role here — its three components span the planes level–velocity, level–acceleration, velocity–acceleration — and biquaternions (Clifford's extension) are the natural language for comparing two such states. SPTLS lives on that substrate, A ⊗ Cl(3,0): differences (A) acting on a geometric state (Cl(3,0)). The point of the substrate is geometric invariance — the comparison of states doesn't depend on the coordinates you happened to record them in.

The intuition is enough here; the formal development lives in the foundational papers.

The operator: one closed-form solve

SPTLS fits a single linear operator M that advances the jet one step, q(t+1) ≈ M·q(t), by ordinary least squares. That's the entire computation — no unit-root pre-testing, no lag-order search, no differencing decision. It keeps everything OLS gives: a closed form, speed, and fully readable coefficients. With the quadratic terms of the jet it captures nonlinear vector fields too.

How M is computed (and decomposed)

Fit. Stack the jets into Q₀, Q₁ and solve M = (Q₁ᵀQ₀)(Q₀ᵀQ₀)⁻¹ — the same normal-equations least squares as OLS, on the jet instead of on lags.

Grade split. Any 3×3 splits into a symmetric and an antisymmetric part, M = ½(M+Mᵀ) + ½(M−Mᵀ); the antisymmetric part is the grade-2 (bivector) content.

Polar split. From the SVD M = UΣVᵀ, the rotor is R = UVᵀ and the stretch P = VΣVᵀ, so M = R·P. Standard linear algebra; nothing exotic.

It forecasts the state, natively

Because the unknown is the jet, one SPTLS step returns level, velocity and acceleration together — not just the next value. Ordinary least squares gives only the level; momentum and curvature come out only indirectly, by differencing the level forecasts (which amplifies noise and need not be coherent). The native state is what you actually want for control and risk — you know whether you are accelerating into or out of a regime — and it is what makes iterated free-run possible: you advance the whole state, not a single scalar (that is how the attractor below gets regenerated).

Reading M: nine numbers, a full geometry

The fitted operator is a white box. Its nine entries — and the eighteen of its polar form M = R·P — carry the complete local geometry of the dynamics, by Clifford grade.

  • Grade-0 (scalar): the trace / isotropic gain — overall growth or decay.
  • Bivector e₁₂: rotation in the level–velocity plane — the oscillation. (In a damped rotation it dominates: ≈ 0.81.)
  • Bivector e₁₃ / e₂₃: couplings to acceleration — often weak, and noise when the process is simple.
  • Pseudoscalar (grade-3) = det M: the oriented volume change of the jet per step — contraction (dissipation) and orientation.

Polar form M = R·P. The rotor R is pure orientation — an axis (which kinematic plane turns) and an angle (how fast: the frequency). The stretch P is the gain: its eigenvalues are the per-direction growth/decay rates that OLS sees as its "abelian shadow." On the damped rotation above, R turns ≈ 81° per step and det M ≈ −0.40 — the jet cell shrinks to 40% and flips orientation each step.

From point estimate to distribution. That rotor/bivector is the empirical fingerprint of the same grade-2 content that the programme's graded characteristic function lifts from a single estimate to a full distributional spectrum — the bridge from "this fit" to "this law."

What the operator says about time

M is the operator that advances the jet of time one step — a local chronometer. Its decomposition reads the texture of time itself: the rotor is time's cyclic, recurrent aspect; the stretch is its persistence and memory; the pseudoscalar (det M < 1) is its irreversibility — the arrow, written as a contraction of phase-space volume. Hamilton called algebra "the science of pure time"; here those nine numbers are an instantaneous algebraic portrait of how time acts on the state.

In practice — three trajectories

Three regimes, judged live in your browser. From a univariate map, to a nonlinear cointegrated pair, to deterministic chaos.

1 · A nonlinear map (the logistic) — OLS vs SPTLS

The logistic map has almost no linear autocorrelation, so an AR/OLS model fails; the quadratic jet recovers the map exactly.

2 · A nonlinear cointegrated pair — VECM/linear vs SPTLS

Two integrated series share an equilibrium, but the equilibrium is a curve, not a line. A linear cointegration / VECM fits a straight line through it; SPTLS on the quadratic jet follows the curve.

3 · Deterministic chaos (Lorenz) — recover the attractor

We fit the 3-D Lorenz state one step ahead, then let each fitted model run free. Only the model that captured the quadratic field regenerates the butterfly; the linear one collapses to a point.

Why SPTLS

SPTLS is a universal approximator of trajectories and dynamical systems: on the kinematic jet, with the graded dictionary, it can represent any smooth vector field — that is why it recovered the Lorenz equations above. Forecasting across every regime follows from this single fact.

Building blocks

The substrate is composable. A ⊗ Cl(3,0) can take a further factor — e.g. ⊗ Cl(0,1) — that adds an extra orientation axis to the state; the least-squares solution doesn't change, only the embedding it runs on. The same machine, retuned to the regime you need.
