Symbolic Pure Time.

An algebraic correction to least squares on time series. Hamilton called algebra "the science of pure time"; a century and a half later, the kinematic jet (y, Δy, Δ²y) lets ordinary least squares read the trajectory as a curve in phase space — forward-looking, geometric, t-free.

One idea: regress on the kinematic jet

Classical time-series models regress on past values (yt-1, yt-2, …). Symbolic Pure-Time Least Squares (SPTLS) regresses on the instantaneous state — the level, its velocity and its acceleration at the same time stamp, the kinematic jet:

q(t) = ( yt, Δyt, Δ²yt )

The two designs span the same column space: with the same number of lags, SPTLS on the jet and OLS on the lag stack produce identical residuals on the level row and identical R². The contribution of SPTLS is not a better fit. It is what the regression returns: an operator M ∈ ℝ3×3 on the jet, instead of a vector of scalar coefficients on the lags. An operator is a richer object — and on the Clifford algebra of the jet, its canonical decomposition reads back the structural geometry of the dynamics.

Why this domain — a thread from Hamilton and Clifford

Two states of a moving system are compared not by subtracting numbers but by the geometric relation that carries one into the other — a rotation-and-stretch. That relation is exactly what quaternions were built to express: Hamilton's i, j, k are the three planes in which a state can turn. The kinematic jet plays the same role here — its three components span the planes level–velocity, level–acceleration, velocity–acceleration — and biquaternions (Clifford's extension) are the natural language for comparing two such states. SPTLS lives on that substrate, A ⊗ Cl(3,0): differences (A) acting on a geometric state (Cl(3,0)). The point of the substrate is that the canonical decomposition of M on this algebra is the structural reading — spectrum, polar, grades — without further post-processing.

The intuition is enough here; the formal development lives in the foundational papers.

The operator: one closed-form solve, three readings

SPTLS fits a single linear operator M that advances the jet one step, q(t+1) ≈ M·q(t), by ordinary least squares. That's the entire computation: a closed-form normal-equations solve. What an econometrician would otherwise run as a sequence of pre-tests (Dickey–Fuller, lag selection, Johansen rank, cross-equation inclusion) is replaced by the fact that the kinematic jet already contains the regressors those tests would have led the analyst to include. The textbook fit is recovered by construction; the structural readings that the textbook workflow extracts as post-fit diagnostics are recovered as canonical decompositions of M.

How M is computed (and decomposed)

Fit. Stack the jets into Q₀, Q₁ and solve M = (Q₁ᵀQ₀)(Q₀ᵀQ₀)⁻¹ — the same normal-equations least squares as OLS, on the jet instead of on lags.

Spectrum. The eigenvalues {λ₁, λ₂, λ₃} of M coincide with the roots of the characteristic polynomial of the equivalent AR(k) fit. They read the order of integration (modulus near 1) and the oscillatory frequency (argument of a complex pair) as properties of the fit object, not as quantities the user has to extract.

Grade split. Any 3×3 splits into a symmetric and an antisymmetric part, M = ½(M+Mᵀ) + ½(M−Mᵀ); the antisymmetric part is the grade-2 (bivector) content.

Polar split. From the SVD M = UΣVᵀ, the rotor is R = UVᵀ and the stretch P = VΣVᵀ, so M = R·P. The rotor angle is the rotation of the dynamics in phase space; det(P) is the per-step volume change.

It forecasts the state, natively

Because the unknown is the jet, one SPTLS step returns level, velocity and acceleration together — not just the next value. Ordinary least squares gives only the level; momentum and curvature come out only indirectly, by differencing the level forecasts (which amplifies noise and need not be coherent). The native state is what you actually want for control and risk — you know whether you are accelerating into or out of a regime — and it is what makes iterated free-run possible: you advance the whole state, not a single scalar (that is how the attractor below gets regenerated).

Reading M: nine numbers, a full geometry

The fitted operator is a white box. Its nine entries — and the eighteen of its polar form M = R·P — carry the complete local geometry of the dynamics, by Clifford grade.

  • Grade-0 (scalar): the trace / isotropic gain — overall growth or decay.
  • Bivector e₁₂: rotation in the level–velocity plane — the oscillation. (In a damped rotation it dominates: ≈ 0.81.)
  • Bivector e₁₃ / e₂₃: couplings to acceleration — often weak, and noise when the process is simple.
  • Pseudoscalar (grade-3) = det M: the oriented volume change of the jet per step — contraction (dissipation) and orientation.

Polar form M = R·P. The rotor R is pure orientation — an axis (which kinematic plane turns) and an angle (how fast: the frequency). The stretch P is the gain: its eigenvalues are the per-direction growth/decay rates that OLS sees as its "abelian shadow." On the damped rotation above, R turns ≈ 81° per step and det M ≈ −0.40 — the jet cell shrinks to 40% and flips orientation each step.

From point estimate to distribution. That rotor/bivector is the empirical fingerprint of the same grade-2 content that the programme's graded characteristic function lifts from a single estimate to a full distributional spectrum — the bridge from "this fit" to "this law."

What the operator says about time

M is the operator that advances the jet of time one step — a local chronometer. Its decomposition reads the texture of time itself: the rotor is time's cyclic, recurrent aspect; the stretch is its persistence and memory; the pseudoscalar (det M < 1) is its irreversibility — the arrow, written as a contraction of phase-space volume. Hamilton called algebra "the science of pure time"; here those nine numbers are an instantaneous algebraic portrait of how time acts on the state.

In practice — four textbook cases

Four trajectories where the naïve OLS baseline a practitioner writes down first is mis-specified or outright spurious, and where the textbook hand-construction recovers the correct fit only after a sequence of pre-tests. In each case, SPTLS on the kinematic jet matches the textbook fit by construction (same column space, same residuals on the level row, same R² and Durbin–Watson) and returns the structural reading as a canonical output of the operator. Each fit runs live in your browser.

1 · Univariate non-stationary AR(2) with one unit root

DGP: yt = 1.6 yt−1 − 0.6 yt−2 + εt. Characteristic roots {1.0, 0.6} — one unit root, one stationary root. Naïve baseline: AR(1). The omitted second lag pushes the residuals into autocorrelation; Durbin–Watson collapses. SPTLS reads the integration order directly off the eigenvalues of M̂.

2 · Trivariate linearly cointegrated VAR

DGP: xt is a random walk; yt = 0.5 yt−1 + 0.5 xt−1 + εy, zt = 0.7 zt−1 + 0.3 xt−1 + εz. Naïve baseline: regress y on z in levels — the classical spurious-regression trap (Granger–Newbold): R² near 1, Durbin–Watson far below 2. SPTLS joint on the kinematic jet recovers the textbook AR-by-equation fit; the cross-block norms of M̂ read the Granger direction.

3 · Univariate oscillatory AR(2) with complex roots

DGP: yt = 2ρ cos(θ) yt−1 − ρ² yt−2 + εt, with ρ = 0.95, θ = π/5. Characteristic roots 0.95 · e±iπ/5. Naïve AR(1) collapses again; the textbook AR(2) fits but reports the cycle as two scalar coefficients that require root-finding to interpret. SPTLS reads the rotational frequency directly as the argument of the complex eigenvalue pair of M̂.

4 · Bivariate rotational VAR(1)

DGP: qt = ρ R(θ) qt−1 + εt with q = (x, y), ρ = 0.95, θ = π/5, and R(θ) a planar rotation. Naïve baseline: regress each series on its own lag only — DW collapses (the rotation lives in the cross-lag). Textbook VAR(1) with cross-lag inclusion recovers the matrix; SPTLS joint extracts the rotation as the polar antisymmetric grade of the level block of M̂joint — one number rather than four coefficients.

Why SPTLS

SPTLS releases OLS from the regimes its backward-looking design was bounded to. On the kinematic jet the fit accommodates non-stationary trends, rotational dynamics and multivariate co-movement without leaving ordinary least squares; and the fitted operator carries — as part of its canonical output — the spectral, polar and graded readings of the dynamics. Interpretability is not sacrificed for coverage; it is enhanced: each guarantee of OLS is preserved, and the structural decomposition of the operator is added on top.

unbounded

Non-stationary trajectories, no pre-test

The kinematic jet absorbs the lag structure a Dickey–Fuller pipeline would impose. Unit roots, near-unit roots and cointegrating relations are part of the regression by construction, not gated by an upstream test.

unbounded

Rotational dynamics, natively

Oscillatory AR(2) processes and rotational VAR(1) couplings are fitted in the same closed-form OLS solve. The rotation does not require differencing or Fourier transforms — it is a property of the operator on the jet.

enhanced

Polar decomposition as native output

The fitted operator factors as M̂ = R̂ · P̂ by SVD — rotation × stretch, delivered as part of the fit. The rotor angle and the principal stretches are canonical, not derived.

enhanced

Grade decomposition by default

Each Clifford grade carries a distinct kinematic role: scalar (isotropic gain), bivector (rotation in each plane), pseudoscalar (oriented volume change). Every coefficient of M̂ sits in a graded slot that names what it does.

What is kept, what is added

Kept: the closed-form solve, the transparent coefficients, Gauss–Markov optimality under the usual regularity, the cheap computational footprint — every property practitioners value about OLS. Added: the spectral reading (order of integration, oscillatory frequency), the polar decomposition (rotation, stretch), the grade decomposition (scalar / bivector / pseudoscalar) and, in the multivariate case, the block structure of M̂joint that reports Granger direction and cointegration rank. Pre-fit batteries (Dickey–Fuller, Johansen, AIC/BIC lag selection) and post-fit batteries (Ljung–Box, periodogram, Granger test) are no longer downstream steps — they are readings of the same operator.

Building blocks

The substrate is composable. A ⊗ Cl(3,0) can take a further factor — e.g. ⊗ Cl(0,1) — that adds an extra orientation axis to the state; the least-squares solution doesn't change, only the embedding it runs on. The same machine, retuned to the regime you need.
