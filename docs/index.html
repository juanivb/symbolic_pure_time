Symbolic Pure Time.

OLS on a time series carries three structural limitations. This removes all three — with one change to the design matrix and the same closed-form solve.

The M-I decomposition ↓ Code & notebooks (MIT)

Three limitations, one fix

Ordinary least squares on time series is the working practitioner's default. But it comes with three structural limitations that are usually addressed by separate, sequential procedures:

Limitation 1 Pre-testing requirement. The regression cannot be set up before you know whether the series is stationary. A Dickey–Fuller battery must run first, and its result determines the specification.
Limitation 2 Residuals as nuisance. The distributional character of the one-step residuals — the actual shape of what arrives as new at each step — is treated as something to validate after the fit, not as a quantity to read from it.
Limitation 3 Two objects, two procedures. The predictable component (what the past determines about the present) and the innovations (what is genuinely new) are recovered separately — one from the fitted coefficients, one from the residual series.

The fix is minimal: instead of regressing on the lagged scalar values (yt−1, yt−2, …), run the same closed-form OLS solve on the kinematic state — level, velocity, acceleration at the same time stamp, stacked as the jet:

q(t) = ( yt, Δyt, Δ²yt )

The two designs span the same column space: SPTLS and OLS on the lag stack produce identical residuals on the level row and identical R². The contribution is not a better fit. What changes is what the regression returns: a 3×3 operator M̂ rather than a vector of scalar coefficients. From that single solve, three simultaneous readings emerge — and all three limitations are removed at once.

Fixed 1 No pre-testing. Integration order is encoded in the eigenvalue spectrum of M̂ and read algebraically in population — the fit is set up correctly for any integration order without a Dickey–Fuller step.
Fixed 2 — the key result Residuals as first-class dynamics. The one-step residuals are the Wold innovation sequence of the kinematic embedding, orthogonal to M̂ by the OLS projection theorem. Their distributional character (captured by the excess kurtosis κ of the velocity-row residuals) is a dynamic reading delivered by the same solve — not a by-product to validate.
Fixed 3 One solve, two channels. The memory axis Δ and the innovation axis κ are orthogonal outputs of the same fit: the M-I decomposition.

The M-I decomposition

The most consequential reading of M̂ is the memory–innovation (M-I) decomposition: two orthogonal axes extracted from the same closed-form solve.

Memory axis Δ — the Frobenius distance between M̂ and the i.i.d. baseline operator M̂iid (fitted on a random permutation of the series, which destroys temporal structure). Δ measures how far the dynamics depart from chance: zero for a white-noise series, large for an integrated or strongly autocorrelated one.

Innovation axis κ — the excess kurtosis of the one-step residuals on the velocity row. κ captures the distributional character of what arrives as genuinely new at each step: near zero for Gaussian innovations, large for heavy-tailed or spike-driven processes.

The two axes are orthogonal: Δ lives in the fitted operator, κ lives in the residuals. They are delivered simultaneously by one OLS solve on the kinematic jet. This is what standard regression does not provide: memory and innovation as two channels of the same fit, rather than as the output of two separate procedures.

The operator: one solve, three readings

SPTLS fits a single linear operator M̂ that advances the jet one step, q(t+1) ≈ M̂·q(t), by ordinary least squares. That's the entire computation: a closed-form normal-equations solve. From that one object, three simultaneous readings emerge.

How M̂ is computed (and decomposed)

Fit. Stack the jets into Q₀, Q₁ and solve M̂ = (Q₁ᵀQ₀)(Q₀ᵀQ₀)⁻¹ — the same normal-equations OLS, on the jet instead of on lags.

M-I decomposition. Δ = ‖M̂ − M̂iid‖F (memory axis); κ = excess kurtosis of velocity-row residuals (innovation axis). The two are orthogonal by the OLS projection theorem.

Polar split. From the SVD M̂ = UΣVᵀ, the rotor is R = UVᵀ and the stretch P = VΣVᵀ. The rotor angle is the rotation of the dynamics in phase space.

Grade decomposition. The Clifford grade structure of M̂ reads isotropic gain (grade-0 / scalar), rotation in each kinematic plane (grade-2 / bivector), and per-step volume change (det M̂ / pseudoscalar).

Reading M̂: nine numbers, a full geometry

The fitted operator is a white box. Its nine entries — and the eighteen of its polar form M̂ = R·P — carry the complete local geometry of the dynamics, by Clifford grade.

  • Grade-0 (scalar): isotropic gain — overall growth or decay.
  • Bivector e₁₂: rotation in the level–velocity plane — the oscillatory component.
  • Bivectors e₁₃ / e₂₃: couplings to acceleration — often weak for simple processes.
  • Pseudoscalar (det M̂): oriented volume change per step — contraction and irreversibility.

Polar form M̂ = R·P. The rotor R is pure orientation — which kinematic plane turns and how fast. The stretch P is the gain: its eigenvalues are the per-direction growth/decay rates.

In practice — four textbook cases

Four trajectories where the naïve OLS baseline is mis-specified, and where the textbook hand-construction recovers the correct fit only after a sequence of pre-tests. In each case, SPTLS on the kinematic jet matches the textbook fit by construction (same column space, same residuals, same R² and Durbin–Watson) and returns the structural reading as a canonical output of the operator. Each fit runs live in your browser.

1 · Univariate non-stationary AR(2) with one unit root

DGP: yt = 1.6 yt−1 − 0.6 yt−2 + εt. Roots {1.0, 0.6}. Naïve AR(1) collapses; SPTLS reads integration order from the eigenvalues of M̂.

2 · Trivariate linearly cointegrated VAR

The classical spurious-regression trap (Granger–Newbold). SPTLS joint recovers the textbook AR-by-equation fit; the cross-block of M̂ reads the Granger direction.

3 · Univariate oscillatory AR(2) with complex roots

DGP: roots 0.95 · e±iπ/5. SPTLS reads the rotational frequency as the argument of the complex eigenvalue pair — no periodogram, no root-finding on the AR polynomial.

4 · Bivariate rotational VAR(1)

DGP: qt = 0.95·R(π/5)·qt−1 + εt. SPTLS joint extracts the rotation as the polar antisymmetric grade of the level block — one number, not four coefficients.

Why SPTLS

SPTLS is not a different estimator. It is ordinary least squares run on a different design matrix. Every property practitioners value in OLS carries over — closed-form solution, Gauss–Markov optimality, cheap computation, transparent coefficients. What extends is the reading the regression returns by default.

fixed

No pre-testing for integration order

The kinematic jet absorbs the lag structure a Dickey–Fuller pipeline would impose. Unit roots and cointegrating relations are part of the regression by construction.

key result

Residuals as first-class dynamics

The one-step residuals are the Wold innovation sequence, orthogonal to M̂ by the projection theorem. Their distributional character (κ) is a dynamic reading of the series, not a validation step run after the fit.

fixed

Memory and innovation from one solve

Δ and κ are two orthogonal outputs of the same OLS solve. Standard regression recovers the predictable component and the innovations as the output of two separate procedures; SPTLS delivers both simultaneously.

enhanced

Polar decomposition as native output

The fitted operator factors as M̂ = R̂·P̂ by SVD — rotation × stretch, delivered as part of the fit. The rotor angle and principal stretches are canonical, not derived.

enhanced

Grade decomposition by default

Each Clifford grade carries a distinct kinematic role: scalar (isotropic gain), bivector (rotation in each plane), pseudoscalar (oriented volume change per step).

multivariate

Block reading: Granger and cointegration

In the multivariate case, the block structure of M̂joint reports Granger direction and cointegration rank from one 3K×3K solve — without separate test batteries.

What is kept, what is added

Kept: the closed-form solve, Gauss–Markov optimality, cheap computational footprint, transparent coefficients and t-statistics. Added: M-I decomposition (memory and innovation as orthogonal channels), spectral reading (integration order, oscillatory frequency), polar decomposition (rotation, stretch), grade decomposition (scalar / bivector / pseudoscalar), and the block structure for multivariate Granger and cointegration. Pre-fit batteries (Dickey–Fuller, Johansen, AIC/BIC lag selection) and post-fit batteries (Ljung–Box, periodogram, Granger test) are no longer downstream steps — they are readings of the same operator.

The result is not a competing methodology so much as a more complete reading of what OLS already does.

Asymptotic theory

The applied readings demonstrated above rest on a formal asymptotic result: the characteristic polynomial of the SPTLS operator M̂ has standard √T-Gaussian asymptotics uniformly across integration orders d ∈ {0, 1, 2}.

The mechanism: rank-one identity → universal Jacobian

Under any DGP, the SPTLS estimation error M̂ − M₀ has a rank-one structure — all three columns of the error matrix are proportional. This forces the Jacobian of the map M ↦ (c₁, c₂, c₃) to be the same upper-triangular Pascal matrix J = [[1,1,1],[0,1,2],[0,0,1]] regardless of the data-generating process. The Jacobian annihilates the Brownian-motion contamination that ordinarily produces non-standard limit theory under integration.

What follows

theorem

√T-normality

The characteristic polynomial coefficients (c₁, c₂, c₃) of M̂ converge to a Gaussian at rate √T for all d ∈ {0, 1, 2} — no Dickey–Fuller tables needed.

corollary

χ²(1) integration tests

Two Wald statistics with textbook χ²(1) critical values: W₁* tests for a unit root (d ≥ 1 vs d = 0), W₂ tests d = 2 vs d = 1. Both use the same estimator and the same asymptotic framework.

extension

AR(p) via Pascal Jacobian

For higher-order AR processes, the universal Jacobian generalises to a Pascal matrix whose entries are binomial coefficients: J(p)k,j = C(j, k−1). The √T-normality result extends to AR(p) for all p.

robustness

HAC under GARCH

Monte Carlo evidence confirms that Newey–West HAC-based versions of W₁* and W₂ maintain correct size under GARCH(1,1) conditional heteroskedasticity.
